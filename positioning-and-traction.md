## Why public sector (one paragraph)

Construction tendering turned out well-served — Constructionline's "X-Ray" already extracts requirements and flags missed items on construction packs (incl. drawings) at ~10k companies, on documents far harder for us to parse. Public-sector ITTs are mostly **text** (lower execution risk for a team with no PDF speciality), the strong incumbent (AutogenAI) is a **generative** tool priced for large orgs (leaving the auditable-extraction end open), and SME public-sector bidders are **under-tooled and hungry** (better traction odds). Lower risk, cleaner gap, hungrier market.

## The gap — what public-sector tools DON'T do well (our wedge)

Two incumbent camps: **generative** (AutogenAI — "AI writes your bid", enterprise-priced) and **library** (Loopio/Responsive — reuse past answers, need a maintained library). The gap is the space between them. In priority order:

1. **Traceability of the extraction itself** *(our spine, most confident gap).* Both camps treat "read the tender → figure out the requirements" as a black box. Neither lets the bid manager verify it: no "requirement 47 → the exact sentence on page 31 it came from, click to check." The 2026 market shift is toward exactly this — trust/trace what was extracted. Matters most in public sector because one wrong requirement loses the whole bid.
2. **The disqualifier catch as a loud, first-class feature.** Public tenders have hard pass/fail gates. Tools bury them in a flat list. None grabs you and says "THIS is mandatory, you don't meet it, missing it disqualifies the entire bid." Our hero moment.
3. **A completeness guarantee — measured recall** *(our second-most-confident gap).* Every tool hands you a list and you *hope* it's complete. None says "found 98%, here are the 2% we're unsure about, flagged." The bid manager's real fear is a *missed* requirement, not a wrong one. Our eval harness solves the exact anxiety others ignore.
4. **Human-in-control compliance workflow** (approve each requirement) rather than "AI writes it for you." Underserved for the compliance step — and it's Conduct's exact thesis.
5. **Decision capture** — the dozens of judgment calls a bid manager makes ("interpret this ambiguously", "no-bid risk") evaporate today. No tool records the decisions + why. Our direct bridge to Conduct's "we capture the context of their decisions."
6. **Requirement → award-criterion → weighting mapping.** Public tenders publish how they score (e.g. quality 60% / price 40% with sub-weightings). Tools extract a flat list but rarely connect each requirement to the criterion and marks it carries. Bidders badly want "where do the most marks live." Natural part of our graph.
7. **Affordability / segment** (positioning, not a feature). AutogenAI is priced for big orgs; the long tail (SME bidders + small bid consultancies) works in Word + spreadsheets. That's the hungry, reachable market.

*Confidence note: #1 and #3 are the gaps we're most sure are real and underserved — and they're our strongest features anyway. Verify the rest on AutogenAI/Loopio feature pages or in the traction calls.*

## Traction — why it's more than a tiebreaker

Henry's words: *"if the product is good, you'll get traction, but it's a week long so it's not a requirement."* Read it precisely — he's saying **traction is evidence the product is good**, the most credible proof for the highest-weighted scores (real process / does it work / saves time), which a 90-second demo can't fully establish. So weight it as a real deliverable.

But the causation is **good product → traction**, not the reverse. So: gate it behind a demo-solid build, and treat it as *proof of quality*, never as hustle to cover a weak build. A stumbling demo shown to a bid manager produces the opposite of proof.

## Who to target (priority order)

1. **Small bid-writing consultancies** *(best target).* Firms whose whole business is responding to tenders — Executive Compass, Thornton & Lowe, Hudson, Bid Solutions, and many smaller ones. They feel the pain daily, handle many bids (value compounds), decide in one conversation, reachable on LinkedIn. One consultancy saying "we'd use this on every bid" beats ten individual bidders. **Start here.**
2. **SMEs that bid regularly for public contracts** — care providers, facilities management, cleaning, catering, IT/managed services, training providers. Under-tooled, hungry. "I'd pilot this on our next NHS framework bid" is gold.

## The motion (J owns; starts ~Day 3–4 once build is demo-solid)

- **Personalised LinkedIn outreach.** Never "buy this" — ask for their expertise:
  > "I've built a tool that breaks a public-sector tender into a requirements checklist, flags the mandatory ones that would disqualify a bid, and links each back to the exact clause so you can verify it. Could I show you on a tender you know and get your honest take on whether you'd use it?"
- **The killer move:** run it **live on a tender they recognise** (a past one, or one they pick). Their reaction to it catching something on a tender they know is the quotable moment.
- **Tier the asks** (capture value at every level):
  - *Floor:* statement of interest — "I'd use this." (Get many.)
  - *Middle:* willing to pilot — "send it when ready, I'll run my next bid through it." (Strong.)
  - *Ceiling:* a pre-commit or tiny paid pilot — even £50–100 from a consultancy is *real money changing hands*. (Ambitious, genuinely possible.)
- **Capture where a judge can see it:** name, role, company, verbatim quote, ideally a short screen-recording. "Four bid managers watched it, here's what they said, one consultancy pilots next week" is a slide almost no team can produce.

## Realistic weekly target

5–8 real conversations · 3–4 statements of interest · 1–2 willing-to-pilot · *if it comes,* 1 micro pre-commit. **Label every tier honestly** — inflating "interested" into "customer" is the one thing that backfires with an ex-Palantir judge who'll probe it.

## The synergy to remember

The people who tell you whether the gap (above) is real are the **same** people who give you traction. Every call both validates/corrects the positioning *and* collects proof. Outreach isn't separate from the build — it's how you de-risk the wedge and gather evidence at once.

## Demo-day framing (ties to Henry's words)

Not "we hustled some signups" → **"we built something good enough that real bid managers wanted it within days — here's the proof."** That makes traction read as *evidence of quality*, exactly how Henry said he reads it.
