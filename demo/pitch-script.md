## Exact Opener - Jawad, Slide 1, Problem, 0:00-0:25

> **"Last year the UK public sector bought hundreds of billions of pounds of goods and services -
> from school meals to cleaning contracts to hospital maintenance. To win that work, a business has
> to answer a tender: basically a very long exam paper from the buyer, saying 'prove you can do this,
> and follow these rules exactly.' It is homework, but with procurement law and worse fonts.**
>
> **Small firms lose it in the stupidest possible way: one mandatory requirement - one non-negotiable
> rule - buried on page 31, missed. The whole bid, weeks of expert work, thrown out unread. That is
> the legacy problem: expert judgment trapped in PDFs, spreadsheets, and one person's head. Bidframe
> exists to move that knowledge into a system the next bid can use."**

NEXT.

> "Let me show you what a tender actually looks like to the person who has to answer it. If you have
> ever opened a PDF and thought, 'this will be quick,' this part is for you."

## Use Case - Jawad, Slide 2, 0:25-0:55

> "This is the job: a thirty, sixty, sometimes hundred-and-fifty-page document, read line by line,
> building a checklist by hand. In tender language, that checklist is the compliance matrix: every
> promise you have to make, every document you have to attach, every rule you have to obey.
>
> Days of senior time per bid, rebuilt from scratch every time. Not exactly the grand digital future;
> more 'one careful person, a spreadsheet, and a very strong cup of tea.'"

NEXT through the slide beats:

> "Bidframe reads first. Deal-breakers surface immediately: the rules where, if you miss them, the
> buyer can reject the bid before reading the rest. Every requirement gets a source trace - the exact
> clause and page - so the checklist is no longer a private spreadsheet with mysterious colour-coding
> only one person understands. It becomes an auditable record.
>
> And where it drafts, it drafts from evidence. The expert stays at the wheel, and their decision
> becomes reusable context for the next bid."

Hand to Bobby:

> "Bobby, show them the bit in the tender where the danger is hiding."

## Ask + Exact Closer - Jawad, Final Slide, 4:25-5:00

> "Conduct's thesis is that legacy moves when the context of expert decisions moves with the work.
> That is exactly what Bidframe captures: every approve, every edit, every flag, every gap answer,
> every evidence link.
>
> The glamorous bit is not the PDF parsing. I know, shocking. The valuable bit is the decision memory.
> The matrix is the surface. The decision record is the moat."

Ask:

> "We are looking for pilot SMEs and design partners. Bring us a public-sector tender; we will have
> the deal-breaker checklist ready before the call. bidframe.org."

Final two sentences, verbatim:

> **"Weeks of expert reading, a disqualifier risk, and a blank page - down to minutes, with the killer
> requirement caught, every line checkable against the document, and a human approving every step.
> We did not build an AI that writes bids; we built the layer that moves legacy bid knowledge out of
> PDFs and memory, into a system people can trust."**

## Delivery Notes

- The three moments that must land: the problem (legacy bid knowledge trapped in PDFs, spreadsheets,
  and heads), Bobby's catch (slide 3's buried disqualifiers), and the source-proof click in the live demo.
- Keep repeating the legacy-move arc: hidden risk -> visible record -> human decision -> reusable context.
- Translate the jargon once, then use it confidently: tender = buyer's long exam paper; mandatory
  requirement = non-negotiable rule; deal-breaker = miss it and the bid can be rejected; compliance
  matrix = the checklist; source trace = click back to the exact page.
- Say "synthetic" with the 101/101. Say "pilots", never "customers". Never a headline recall percentage.
